Founder technical note

Why OTRUST exists.

In research on cognition I kept running into the problem: how do you prove something existed before a certain date without sharing the idea? OTRUST is the answer — local-first, hash-only, anchored to Bitcoin.

Kris Ledel Updated May 27, 2026 Blind notary architecture

Verification before platform

Most product trust problems are not solved by asking users to believe another dashboard. They are solved by producing artifacts that can be checked later: hashes, signatures, timestamps, ID packages, and callback records.

OTRUST starts from those artifacts. The UI exists to make ID creation usable, but the core object is the record that survives the UI.

Blind notarization

The timestamping flow hashes content in the browser. The service receives the hash, signature, public key, timestamps, and record metadata. It does not need the raw document to verify that the document existed.

  • The user keeps the original file.
  • The public record can still be verified later.
  • The service minimizes what it must know to be useful.

Anchors and independence

OpenTimestamps gives OTRUST a path to external anchoring without pretending that a hosted service alone is the source of truth. A record can be useful immediately and stronger once it is anchored externally.

ID-first identity

Identity is not only login. In B2B flows, the important question is often narrower: does this user control an approved ID, and can the relying party verify that fact without seeing more personal data than needed?

That is why OTRUST ID and hosted Auth keep the provider boundary visible. The partner experience can be branded, but the ID processor should remain clear.

Why the boundary matters

A trust product should not hide who performs the trust-sensitive step. OTRUST keeps URLs, disclosures, ID links, and privacy notes explicit so users know when they are interacting with OTRUST and when they return to a partner.
